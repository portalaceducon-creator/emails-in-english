📚CONCEPT · What Is a Business Email?
Definition: Business Email
A business email is a formal or semi-formal written communication used in professional contexts to exchange information, make requests, confirm agreements, manage relationships, and conduct commercial transactions. Unlike personal messages, every business email must reflect professionalism, clarity, and respect for the reader.
  • Creates a permanent written record — every word can be saved, forwarded, or printed
  • Represents not just you, but your entire organisation
  • Requires careful choice of register, tone, and vocabulary
  • Over 300 billion emails are sent worldwide every single day
📐CONCEPT · Register in Email
What Is Register?
Register is the level of formality you choose when writing. Using the wrong register is one of the most common and damaging mistakes. Always match your language to your audience and context.
RegisterUse WhenOpening ExampleSign-off
🔵 FormalNew clients, executives, legalDear Mr./Ms. [Surname],Yours sincerely,
🟡 Semi-FormalRegular contacts you knowDear [First Name], / Hi Sarah,Best regards,
🟢 InformalClose colleagues, internalHey Mike, / Hi!Thanks, / Best,

🔬CONCEPT · Email Structure
The 8-Part Email Architecture
Every professional email contains exactly 8 key components. Understanding the function of each one gives you a reusable template for any situation. A missing component creates confusion; a poorly written one creates a negative impression.
  • Each component has a specific function and position
  • Components must appear in the correct sequence
  • The opening line and CTA are the two most frequently written badly
1
SUBJECT LINE
Determines whether the email is opened. Must be specific, clear, under 50 characters, and action-oriented. Write it FIRST.
"Budget Approval Required — Project Alpha — Action by 31 Oct"
2
SALUTATION / GREETING
Sets the tone immediately. Always verify the person's correct name, title, and preferred form of address.
"Dear Mr. Henderson," / "Hi Anna," / "Dear Sir or Madam,"
3
OPENING LINE
States your reason for writing in sentence ONE. Never leave the reader guessing. Apply the BLOT method.
"I am writing to request an extension on the Q3 report submission."
4
BODY — PARAGRAPH 1
Context or background. Maximum 2–3 sentences. One idea per paragraph only — never mix topics.
"As discussed last Tuesday, the project timeline has been affected by unforeseen circumstances."
5
BODY — PARAGRAPH 2
Main message: the core request, information, or explanation. This is the most important paragraph.
"We would therefore like to request an extension of ten business days."
6
CALL TO ACTION (CTA)
Tells the reader exactly WHAT to do, WHO must do it, and BY WHEN. One specific action per email.
"Could you please confirm your approval by Friday 3 November, 5pm?"
7
CLOSING LINE
A polite sentence that offers further assistance, sets expectations, or expresses goodwill before the sign-off.
"Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require any further information."
8
SIGN-OFF + SIGNATURE BLOCK
Farewell phrase + full name + job title + company + direct phone. Never just your first name alone.
"Kind regards, / James Park | Marketing Director | TechFlow UK | +44 20 7946 1234"

Step-by-Step Writing Process
⏱ 25 min · 7 Steps · BLOT · Subject Lines · Planning Grid

🔄CONCEPT · Writing as a Process
Why Writing Is a Process, Not a Single Action
Professional writers never just type and send. They plan → draft → revise → proofread → send. Even experienced writers follow a structured process because it produces clearer communication and avoids costly mistakes.
⚡CONCEPT · The BLOT Method
BLOT = Bottom Line On Top
In business writing, the most important information always comes first. Business readers are busy — they skim. If your key message is buried in paragraph 3, it may never be read.
❌ Without BLOT
"I hope you are well. Following our meeting... [2 paragraphs]... I need the report by Friday."
✅ With BLOT
"I am writing to request the Q3 report by Friday 5pm. [context follows]"
🏷CONCEPT · The Subject Line
Why Subject Lines Are the Most Important Line You Write
Research shows 47% of recipients decide whether to open an email based solely on the subject line. A specific, well-crafted subject line gets your email opened, prioritised, and acted on.
  • Include: topic + context + date or action keyword
  • Maximum: ~50 characters for full display on mobile
  • Avoid: "Hi", "Update", "FYI", RE:RE:RE:, ALL CAPS, excessive !!!
  • Formula: [Topic] — [Context/Name] — [Date or Action]
1
DEFINE YOUR PURPOSE
Write your goal in one sentence BEFORE typing anything else. Ask: What should the reader DO or KNOW?
  • Am I informing, requesting, apologising, confirming, or complaining?
  • Who is my reader? What is their seniority and our relationship?
2
WRITE THE SUBJECT LINE FIRST
A strong subject line is written before the body — it keeps you focused.
  • ❌ "Update" → ✅ "Q3 Report: Budget Revised — Action Required by 31 Oct"
  • Include: topic + context + date or required action
3
CHOOSE YOUR SALUTATION
Match formality to the recipient. Never assume gender from a first name alone.
  • "Dear Dr./Mr./Ms. [Surname]" — formal or senior contact
  • "Hi [First Name]" — established colleague or regular contact
4
WRITE YOUR OPENING LINE (BLOT)
State your purpose in sentence one. Delete anything that doesn't add direct value.
  • ✅ "I am writing to request an extension for the Q3 submission."
  • ❌ "I hope this email finds you well. How have you been?" — slow start!
5
BUILD THE BODY (ONE IDEA PER PARAGRAPH)
Para 1: Context. Para 2: Main message. Para 3: Supporting detail. 2–4 sentences each.
  • Use bullet points for 3+ listed items — never for 1 or 2 things
  • White space improves readability dramatically
6
ADD A SPECIFIC CALL TO ACTION
Tell the reader: WHO does WHAT by WHEN. Ask for ONE action per email.
  • ✅ "Please review and sign the contract by Friday, 5pm."
  • ❌ "Let me know what you think." — too vague, no deadline
7
PROOFREAD — E.M.A.I.L. CHECKLIST
E=Errors · M=Manner/Tone · A=Attachments · I=Identify recipients · L=Logic
  • Use spell-check AND read aloud — your ear catches what your eyes miss
  • For sensitive emails: write → wait 24h → reread → send

📂CONCEPT · Email Categories
Why Identifying the Email Type Matters
Each type of email has its own purpose, tone expectations, structure, and vocabulary. Identifying the type before you write helps you choose the right opening, correct formality level, and appropriate call to action. Mixing types creates confusion and unprofessionalism.
  • Request emails need polite, indirect language
  • Apology emails need the Acknowledge → Explain → Resolve → Reassure formula
  • Complaint emails need to be firm but never aggressive
📋REQUEST
Asking for information, approval, a meeting, a document, or a decision.
  • "I am writing to request a copy of…"
  • "I would like to request your approval for…"
✅CONFIRMATION
Confirming a meeting, booking, order, agreement, or receipt of a document.
  • "I am writing to confirm our meeting on…"
  • "As agreed, I am pleased to confirm…"
😔APOLOGY
Acknowledging an error. Formula: Acknowledge + Explain + Resolve + Reassure.
  • "I am writing to sincerely apologise for…"
  • "I take full responsibility for the delay in…"
📢ANNOUNCEMENT
Sharing news: product launches, policy changes, events, updates.
  • "I am pleased to announce that…"
  • "We would like to inform you of an important change…"
🤝INTRODUCTION
Introducing yourself, a colleague, or a product to a new contact.
  • "My name is [X] and I am the [role] at…"
  • "I was referred to you by [name]…"
📌FOLLOW-UP
Checking in after a meeting, proposal, interview, or unanswered email.
  • "I am following up on my email of [date]…"
  • "I wanted to check in after our meeting…"
❌COMPLAINT
Formally raising a concern. Firm but professional. Always propose a solution.
  • "I am writing to formally raise a concern regarding…"
  • "I am disappointed to report that…"
🙏THANK-YOU
Expressing sincere gratitude after a meeting, interview, or collaboration.
  • "I am writing to thank you for…"
  • "I wanted to express my sincere gratitude for…"

📖CONCEPT · Fixed Expressions in Business English
Why Professional Email Has Its Own Language
Business email English uses many fixed expressions — phrases that native speakers use repeatedly because they are efficient, appropriately polite, and universally understood. Learning these phrases gives you a ready-made toolkit for any professional situation without having to invent language from scratch.
  • They signal that you understand business communication conventions
  • They are culturally neutral and professionally safe across cultures
  • They save time and reduce the risk of accidentally sounding rude
📏CONCEPT · The Politeness Scale
Making Requests — From Too Direct to Too Passive
In English, there is a spectrum of politeness when making requests. Too direct sounds rude; too passive sounds weak and unprofessional. Your target is the polite, clear professional zone.
PhraseLevelWhen to Use
"Send me the file."❌ Too DirectAlmost never in professional email
"Please send the file."⚠️ Direct but OKQuick internal messages only
"Could you please send me the file?"✅ Polite StandardMost everyday business emails
"I would appreciate it if you could send…"✅✅ Very PoliteFormal or senior contact
"Would it be possible for you to send…"✅✅✅ Most TentativeSensitive requests, first contact

⚠️CONCEPT · The Real Cost of Email Mistakes
Why Mistakes in Professional Emails Are Never "Just Small Errors"
A poorly written email can lose a client, damage a relationship, create legal problems, or end a career. In a study by Grammarly, 72% of business leaders said writing quality significantly impacts their perception of an employee's professionalism. Every email you send is a professional statement about you and your organisation.
  • Emails are permanent — they can be screenshotted, forwarded, and used as evidence
  • In global business, poor English signals lack of care, not just language difficulty
  • The 8 mistakes below are the most common and most damaging in professional communication
MISTAKE 1 — Vague Subject Line
❌ Common Mistake
"Hi" / "Update" / "Question" / "FYI" / "URGENT!!!"
✅ Better Version
"Project Alpha — Revised Budget — Approval Required by 31 Oct"
💡 Vague subjects = #1 reason emails go unopened. Formula: Topic + Context + Date or Action.
MISTAKE 2 — Wrong Register / Tone
❌ Common Mistake
"Hey!! Can u send me the doc ASAP?? Thx!!!"
✅ Better Version
"Dear James, Could you please send the document at your earliest convenience?"
💡 Match formality to the relationship. Slang, abbreviations, and !!! are unprofessional.
MISTAKE 3 — No Clear Purpose or Call to Action
❌ Common Mistake
"Just checking in. Let me know what you think." (About what? By when?)
✅ Better Version
"I am writing to request your feedback on the attached proposal by Friday 5pm."
💡 Every email needs ONE clear purpose and ONE specific action. State it in sentence one.
MISTAKE 4 — Wall of Text
❌ Common Mistake
One massive paragraph covering 5 topics with no breaks or structure.
✅ Better Version
One paragraph = one idea. Use bullet points for 3+ items. White space = readability.
💡 Business readers skim. If they can't find your point in 10 seconds, they move on.
MISTAKE 5 — Missing or Misnamed Attachments
❌ Common Mistake
"Please see the attached report for details." [No attachment sent]
✅ Better Version
Attach the file FIRST. Name it: "ProjectX_Report_Nov2024.pdf" not "final_v3.docx"
💡 Pro tip: attaching the file is Step Zero — do it before writing a single word.
MISTAKE 6 — Emotional or Aggressive Language
❌ Common Mistake
"I'm really frustrated that this keeps happening. It's completely unacceptable!"
✅ Better Version
"I am concerned that this issue has recurred. Could we discuss a long-term solution?"
💡 24-hour rule: write when angry, wait 24h, reread, THEN decide whether to send.
MISTAKE 7 — Reply All Misuse
❌ Common Mistake
Replying "Thanks!" to a company-wide email with 200+ people in the CC field.
✅ Better Version
Always ask: does EVERYONE on this thread need this reply? If no — click Reply only.
💡 Reply All misuse is one of the fastest ways to damage your professional reputation.
MISTAKE 8 — Grammar & Spelling Errors
❌ Common Mistake
"Thank you for you're time. I look foward to meet you."
✅ Better Version
"Thank you for your time. I look forward to meeting you."
💡 Use spell-check AND read aloud — your ear catches what your eyes miss.

⚖️CONCEPT · Professional Tone in Email
Why Tone Is So Easy to Get Wrong in Writing
In face-to-face communication, 55% of meaning comes from body language and 38% from voice tone. In email, 100% of your meaning comes from words alone. This makes tone extremely easy to misread. The same message can sound polite, neutral, or aggressive depending on word choice alone.
  • Read every email as if you ARE the recipient — would you feel respected?
  • Avoid passive-aggressive phrases: "As I mentioned before…" / "As you should know…"
  • Negative emotions + email = danger. Write → wait → reread → send
✅ DO THIS — Best Practices
Respond within 24 hours — or send an acknowledgement
One email = one clear topic or request
Name attachments: "ProjectX_Report_Nov2024.pdf"
Use Bcc for large lists (protect privacy)
Wait 24h before sending emotional emails
Include a full signature block in every email
Ask for ONE specific action with a clear deadline
❌ AVOID THIS
"Just checking in" with no clear purpose
Walls of text without paragraph breaks
Files named "final_FINAL_v3_use_this_one.docx"
Reply All to large groups unnecessarily
Sending angry emails in the heat of the moment
Signing off as just "Thanks" on formal first emails
Asking for 5 different things in one email

✅ Pre-Send Checklist — 10 Points

Subject line is specific, clear, and under 50 characters
Salutation matches the required level of formality
Opening line states the purpose immediately (BLOT)
Body is structured: one idea per paragraph
Bullet points used for 3+ items only
Call to action is specific: WHAT + WHO + BY WHEN
Tone is professional — would you be comfortable if your boss saw this?
Spelling and grammar have been proofread
All mentioned attachments are included and named correctly
Sending to the correct people — Reply vs Reply All?

🔍CONCEPT · Analysing Model Emails
How to Learn from Professional Email Examples
Reading and analysing model emails is one of the fastest ways to improve your own writing. As you read each example, actively look for: the email type, the register, the opening line structure (BLOT), the call to action, the closing phrase, and any vocabulary you want to use in your own emails.

📧 Example 1 — Formal Request Email

✉ Formal Request — Deadline Extension
To:j.henderson@globalcorp.com
Cc:m.patel@globalcorp.com
Subject:Request for Extension — Q3 Financial Report — New Deadline: 20 October

Dear Mr. Henderson,

I am writing to request a five-day extension for the submission of the Q3 Financial Report, originally due on 15 October 2024.

Due to unforeseen delays in receiving data from our regional offices, our team requires additional time to ensure the accuracy and completeness of the report.

We anticipate delivering the finalised document no later than 20 October. I sincerely apologise for any inconvenience and greatly appreciate your understanding.

Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require any further information.

Yours sincerely,
Sarah Mitchell | Financial Analyst | Apex Solutions Ltd | +44 20 7946 0958

📧 Example 2 — Apology Email

✉ Apology Email — Late Delivery
To:c.taylor@northstarlegal.com
Subject:Formal Apology — Delayed Contract Documents — Case #CF-2024-091

Dear Ms. Taylor,

I am writing to sincerely apologise for the delay in delivering the signed contract documents, which were due on 10 November 2024.

The delay was caused by an internal review process that took longer than anticipated. I take full responsibility for this oversight and the inconvenience it has caused your team.

I am pleased to confirm that the documents have now been signed and will be couriered to your office today for delivery by 9:00 AM tomorrow, 13 November.

We value your partnership greatly and I assure you this will not happen again.

Yours sincerely,
David Osei | Operations Manager | Meridian Consulting | +44 20 8123 4567

⭐ Key Takeaways — Remember These Always
📌Structure every email: Subject · Greeting · Opening · Body · CTA · Closing · Sign-off
📌Match your register (formal / semi-formal / informal) to your reader and context every time
📌Subject lines determine whether your email gets opened — make them specific and clear
📌BLOT: Bottom Line On Top — state your purpose in sentence one, always
📌One email = one topic. One paragraph = one idea. Bullets for 3+ items only.
📌Your CTA must answer: WHO does WHAT by WHEN — never be vague about action
📌Proofread with E.M.A.I.L.: Errors · Manner · Attachments · Identify · Logic
📌Tone in email = 100% words only — choose them with care every single time
📌The 24-hour rule: write → wait → reread → send for any sensitive or important email
📌Your email represents you and your entire organisation — every word counts
